SEO managers keep telling me the same thing: leadership is panicking about LLM visibility. You already know my opinion on that panic. Not the point of this post. The point is what citation should actually mean for a brand once you stop chasing the panic and start thinking about what good looks like. If your brand has done its job in the market, your homepage should be the answer when someone asks an LLM for the best option in your category. Not a comparison page buried six clicks deep or a blog post. The homepage. That's a different rule than the one SEOs grew up on where the goal was breadth. AEO doesn't work like that, and it shouldn't, because AEO is closer to a branding channel than a traffic channel. Look at the Semrush Enterprise report, and you'll see Indeed living proof of this. Their homepage is the main driver of their AI visibility. Not a help article. Not a landing page built for some long-tail query. The homepage is doing the job a homepage is supposed to do. Indeed's SEO team isn't winning that slot through some clever AEO tactic. They're cashing a check that brand marketing wrote years ago. So before anyone on your team budgets for a new tool or starts a new "AEO initiative," stop and look at what you've already got. Where does your brand show up right now, and is it the page you'd actually want representing you? That gap between your baseline and your ambition is where you justify new spend.
